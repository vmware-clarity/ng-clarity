Onboarding is a set of workflows or prompts that actively guide users to continously find new values in your product. The value of a product is often referred as a user’s aha moment. The product/feature onboarding experience extends throughout the user lifecycle. A good onboarding experience can increase conversation rate, retention rate, customer satisfaction, and reduce support requests.

Usage

  • When the users are new to the product or features and ready to learn more.
  • For complex products that require some initial guidance to get up to speed.
  • To show the product value and how it works in context.
  • When the users are experienced and there is nothing new to learn.
  • For self-explanatory products that the users can learn without any guidance.
  • To show marketing materials again and educate the users what the product does.
  • To upsell additional services to the customer, as that would erode trust in the onboarding being useful to what they do now.

Intuitive UI - User Onboarding

Intuitive UI and onboarding share a similar goal to make your product easier to understand for users. However, they cannot replace each other. Optimized UI is intuitive for daily operations. However, some UI doesn’t always do a good job of introducing users' key features and values This is where product/features onboarding comes in, to lead the users from the start until they are up and running in the product.

Scenarios

There are three common scenarios to use an onboarding: initial product setup, the first time in trial/product, and the first time since major updates.

Initial Product Setup

Some products require initial product setup to enable the product for all other users.

  • Intended for system admins who installed or created the account for their organizations.
  • Guide the admins through the complex setup process to match their specific user cases.
  • Focus on the key tasks that the admins would want to unblock for their users.

First Time in Trial or Product

It is a great opportunity to welcome your users, and show them how your products can help them solve their problems.

  • Trial users sign up to evaluate the product value with respect to the price. Keep it fast, lightweight, and with minimal user input.
  • First time users are looking to properly set up and get up to speed with their day to day operations.
  • Focus on the top 3-5 tasks that best showcase the key value proposition of the product. You don’t have to show them everything in the onboarding.

First Time Since Major Updates

Onboarding can continue even when a user is already fully up to speed since your product will also be evolving.

  • Existing users who are more experienced with your product would like to learn new features to continue realizing the product’s value.
  • Give them an overview of the new features and benefits. Your users are not likely to keep checking your marketing materials after purchasing your product.
  • Show them how the new feature works.
  • Be mindful of the timing and how you show them the new features. Don’t force it to your users as they might be in the middle of their daily tasks.

How to Design an Onboarding Experience

Pick one or more patterns based on your use cases.

Welcome the User

Welcome the user and makes a positive first impression. Use a welcome message at the beginning of a product or feature onboarding as an opportunity to start building trust and show value in the product. Make the user feel supported and inspired to explore the content. It can be combined with a product overview or announcement of new features, with a call to action to learn and explore. It can also include illustrations or a featured video highlighting the new features. The welcome message can be shown in a modal, a dismissable card, or in page. See examples in Figma.

Personalize for multiple personas. Your users might have different roles, different goals, different level of domain knowledge and experience with your product. Personalization can make it more relavant to their specific needs. Also, consider what the percentage of each persona is, whether you need to focus on one over the others. See examples in figma.

  • When you need to grab user’s immediate attention
  • For brief system announcement or updates overview
  • When you need to ask for information that might impact the following workflow, for example, user roles and goals
  • When it’s ok for the user to take their own pace to consume the content
  • For longer onboarding workflows that the user might not complete in one session

Guide the User Through Key Tasks

There are a few patterns to consider based on your use cases.

Empty States

Empty states is placeholder messages that tell users that there’s no content yet, and more importantly, what they can do next.

Use empty states to give users a preview of, or value proposition for a future experience. It provides an integrated experience that shows the users how valuable this area can be and get them started by providing a clear call to action.

Empty states consist of a title (optional), a message, a call to action, and illustration (optional). Empty states can be placed in the container or layout. See examples in figma.

  • To encourage users to explore on their own
  • To leverage the screen real estate that the content will show up and guide the user in place in a less obtrusive way
  • If the users are not likely to land the page by themselves. Consider a more proactive pattern, such as a product walkthrough
  • To simply report that “There is not data to display” or even condemn the users “You have not done anything yet”

Product Walkthrough

Product walkthrough is one or a series of popover elements that provides contextual information to the users. Use a product walkthrough to show key UI elements or guide through critical workflows. It may be triggered by a user initiation, or may simply appear the first time a user arrives on a new or updated page. See examples in figma.

There are two types of product walkthroughs:

UI Walkthrough

  • Highlights key features in place to help users familiarize with the UI
  • Use to introduces new features, or point out significant changes.
  • It is recommended to include no more than 5 items at a time.

Workflow Walkthrough

  • Action-driven and a step-by-step guide to achieve a specific goal
  • It can be used on a single page or across multiple pages.
  • To highlight the location of core features or guide the user step by step in context to showcase your product’s core value
  • To show non-critical details since user can learn about them later
  • With too many steps and force the user to complete all at once

Custom-Created Experience

Custom created onboarding experience is a set of dedicated widgets or screens created for the key onboarding tasks. Use it to help users complete the basic setup in one place before the user starts interacting with the regular product UI. This can be used for setup, guidance or information for the users. See the pattern Multi-step workflows for more details.

  • To consolidates all important steps in one place for first time users
  • To reduce friction of complex configurations which can be difficult for users to complete on their own
  • For simple setup, this can be an overkill.
  • For tasks that are centrally located in the UI, a product tour can better alternative

Learn at Their Own Pace

An onboarding experience should empower users, not control them. Users should be able to learn at their own pace.

  • Let the user control the onboarding and move on to the next step when they are ready.
  • Give the user an option to skip a part or whole of the onboarding. If the user has skipped the onboarding, keep it hidden until the user invokes it.
  • Allow the user to quit halfway and revisit later.

Celebrate Achievement

Let users know that they are doing great by acknowledging their key milestones and achievement. It provides users a positive reinforcement and keeps them motivated, building a stronger emotional connection and encouraging long-term engagement from your users. The major milestones could be creating the first object, completing a critical system configuration, or resolving the first ticket. However, don't abuse it as it might create the opposite effects. See examples in Figma.

Revisit the Onboarding

Users might need to revisit the onboarding content after their first completion. Consider providing access to revisit the onboarding. Inform the user at the end of the onboarding upon the first completion so that they know where to access them again for higher proficiency with your product.
